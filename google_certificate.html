The Problem Planning a trip can be time-consuming, overwhelming, and expensive‚Äîespecially for students or financially conscious users. Many people lack the time, tools, or travel knowledge to plan an affordable and enjoyable trip. Others want to travel but don't have companions or can't afford to go solo.

The Goal Design an easy-to-use app that uses AI to simplify trip planning by generating trip ideas and itineraries based on user input. The app should also support social features like group trip planning and cost-sharing, helping users travel more affordably and collaboratively.

User Research To understand user frusturations, needs, and requirements, I conducted informal interviews and secondary research to understand how people, especially students and young professionals plan trips. Key patterns emerged around budget constraints, analysis paralysis, lack of time, and wanting to travel with others but struggling to organize group trips. For this user research, I chose interviews in order to have a deep undertanding of the users pain points.

Starting the Design üé®

Paper wireframes, Digital Wireframes, Low-fidelity Prototype, Usability Studies


Paper Wireframes I began by sketching paper wireframes on my tablet, focusing on the core features identified during user research. For each screen, I explored three different layout ideas. From these, I selected my favorite elements to create a consolidated final draft.



I then used Figma to translate my sketches into low-fidelity wireframes. After several iterations, these screens best represented the user flow and addressed the key needs uncovered in research. From the finalized wireframes, I built a low-fidelity prototype to test functionality‚Äîprioritizing intuitive flow, accessibility, and usability‚Äîbefore moving on to the final design.

Usability Study: Main Findings & Iterations ‚úçÔ∏èüîÑ

1. Enhance Filters with Granular Options

  • Finding: Users struggled to narrow down trips and friend groups because filter options were too broad.
  • Iteration: Add more detailed filters‚Äîsuch as budget sliders, preferred activities (e.g., hiking, culinary), group size, and traveler age ranges. Implement multi-select checkboxes and real-time filter counts so users immediately see how many results match their criteria.

    • 2. Streamline Navigation Between ‚ÄúFind Friends‚Äù and ‚ÄúBrowse Trips‚Äù
  • Finding: Participants found it confusing to jump from discovering travel buddies to exploring trip ideas‚Äîand couldn‚Äôt easily switch back.
  • Iteration: Introduce a persistent bottom-bar toggle or segmented control that lets users switch seamlessly between ‚ÄúFriends‚Äù and ‚ÄúTrips‚Äù views without losing their current search context. Include breadcrumb-style headers (‚ÄúTrips ‚Ä∫ Tokyo Adventure‚Äù) so users always know where they are.

    • 3.Improve Discovery & Differentiation of Friend Groups
  • Finding: Users had difficulty understanding the differences between various friend groups (e.g., size, cost-shares, compatibility).
  • Iteration: Redesign the groups list with clear labels and icons for key attributes: group size, average budget per person, trip type, and member interests. Add a ‚ÄúPeek Inside‚Äù preview card showing member profiles and recent trip photos to help users choose the best match.

    Reflections & Takeaways üìù


  • Embracing the Design Process from Paper to Prototype Starting with simple paper sketches helped me stay grounded in user needs before jumping into digital tools. It taught me the value of iterating slowly and building each screen with purpose and clarity.
  • Overcoming Challenges with Figma & Functionality Learning to make the app functional in Figma was both rewarding and challenging. I iterated several times to ensure smooth user flows, and each step helped me better understand how structure, interaction, and accessibility all play a role in great design.
  • Growing from User Feedback Conducting usability interviews was a major growth moment. I learned to separate my personal feelings from design critiques, using feedback as a powerful tool to improve the user experience. It reminded me that good design is about solving problems, not defending decisions.
