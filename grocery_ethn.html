Redesigning Grocery Shopping for Young Adults:
A UX Research Approach

  • Back

OVERVIEW

This project explores how young adults (18-34) navigate grocery shopping—whether through in-store visits, pickup, or delivery. By applying an ethnographic lens, we uncovered behavioral patterns, decision-making processes, and digital tool usage that influence their shopping experience. Given their tech-savviness, young adults often approach grocery shopping in diverse and evolving ways, making them a key demographic for understanding future trends in grocery retail.


Project Details

My Role: UX Research | Team Member
Duration: 2 months
Tools: Miro
Skills: Interviews, Affinity Mapping, Laddering

PHASE 1 - UNDERSTAND

Background Research Grocery shopping follows five key stages—problem recognition, search, evaluation, purchase, and outcomes. While online platforms offer pricing flexibility and convenience, our research explored how young adults balance digital and in-store decision-making. Interestingly, despite the rise of e-commerce, we found that online sources had minimal influence on in-store shopping behavior, challenging assumptions about digital adoption

Interviews Interviews with all participants were conducted at a grocery store of their choice. The interview process included questions prepared for before shopping, during shopping, and after shopping. With consent, we recorded participant responses for analysis. During the interviews, participants carried out their grocery shopping while the interviewer followed and asked questions. A post-shopping interview captured their overall experience and demographic details

PHASE 2 - ANALYSIS

We used Miro to centralize and analyze our raw data, incorporating observations, photos, and interview notes from all team members. To uncover key themes, we conducted three iterations of affinity mapping, that allowed us to identify patterns by clustering related insights. This iterative approach allowed us to refine our understanding, ensuring that emerging themes were robust and representative of user experiences. This approach was crucial in distilling complex qualitative data into clear pain points and behavioral trends, enabling us to derive actionable insights that directly informed our design recommendations.


After synthesizing insights from our affinity mapping, we identified key pain points and engaged in an unrestricted brainstorming session to explore potential solutions. We initially explored metaphor extremes, and contrasts for ideation, but found these methods misaligned with our research goals. Instead, we adopted the laddering method, which provided a more structured framework for connecting observed behaviors to underlying user needs. This method helped us develop targeted, actionable design recommendations grounded in our research findings.

PHASE 3 - Recommendations

We developed both long-term and short-term recommendations aimed at enhancing the grocery shopping experience. Our suggestions were crafted to address specific issues identified during our research, with a focus on practical implementation for improving overall user satisfaction.

Short-Term Reccomendation

  • Child Care Assistance → Addressing shopping challenges for parents
  • Wayfinding Improvements → Enhancing in-store navigation
  • Chatbot Support → Streamlining online grocery assistance

    • Long-Term Reccomendation

  • Smart Fridge Integration → Automating grocery management
  • Smart Cart Technology → Enhancing in-store shopping efficiency
